Lesson 1.1 - Notes In Western Music

Types of notes

Western music theory consists of 12 notes, 7 natural notes and 5 accidentals notes.
It is important to become acquainted with these two symbols - # and b.
# = sharp, b = flat
Natural Notes: A - B - C - D - E - F - G
Accidental Notes: A#/Bb - C#/Db - D#/Eb - F#/Gb - G#/Ab
Accidentals have two name for reasons that we will talk about when we get to scales

The Piano is an excellent tool for illustrating and remembering the arrangement of notes.
As you have probably noticed the white keys are all natural notes and the black keys are all accidentals. So you would read the notes ascending like so:
C - (C#/Db) - D - (D#/Eb) - E - F - (F#/Gb) - G - (G#/Ab) - A - (A#/Bb) - B - C

Octaves

You are probably wondering "but what about all the other notes on the piano?"
The piano segment shown above is just one octave and the piano contains multiple octaves.
So what is an octave?

The image above shows a piano and it highlights all of the C notes.
But what makes them different? Frequency, the difference between a C4 and a C5 is a double in frequency.
Ultimately though the note remains the same, it just sounds like a "higher" or "lower version of another."
The note with a number next to it i.e.: C3, is known as "scientific pitch notation". If you ever see these
in the future it specifically tells you what pitch of C you need.
