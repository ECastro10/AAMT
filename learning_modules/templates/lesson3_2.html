Major Scale

Alright time for our second scale.

How to create a major scale

Remember how we created our chromatic scales? Just simply move up by one semitone/half step. The Major Scale takes
a bit more thinking. So first of all, each guitar fret is a semitone, moving up one or down is a half step,
moving two semitones/two frets == tone/whole step.
Having established those basic building blocks here is the formula for any major scale.
(Whole step - Whole step - half step - Whole step - Whole step - Whole step - half step) == (W - W - h - W - W - W - h)

C Major Scale

Knowing the formula, let's learn our C Major Scale.

Now let's look at how this translates on to the fretboard.

Major Scale 6th String Root

Okay, same approach as the chromatic scale with one difference. Notice how the whole scale falls within
a four fret area? So what do you finger do you begin on the root note (friendly reminder, you begin on the
bottom most row, because that is the 6th/low E string)? You want to place your middle finger on that root note.
Why? It behooves you to do so because once you reach the 5th/A string you need that pointer finger to hit a fret
lower than your root note. Another friendly reminder: This shape is mobile, that means that you can play it anywhere
on the fretboard, so long as you have the frets available to play the scale, this includes the open strings.

Major Scale 5th Root

From here on out you will probably get two versions of the same scale. The second version of the scale will be the
same scale but it will begin from the 5th/A string in order to give you more options. Practice both equally.

Okay before you freak out about how many frets this covers let's go over some best practices for guitar playing.
First and foremost, these rules are here to help you out, but remember, this is music, we go over the rules
to break them later one when we know what we are doing. Secondly, you want to avoid playing two back to back different
notes with the same finger. That is to say we will maneuver our fingers to avoid making one finger do a lot of work.

How To Play It!
Remember how on the chromatic scale your hand position slid down one fret in order to play the four notes on the next
string? When it comes to these patterns more than four fret patterns we will almost always change hand position.
Same concept as the one above, start with your middle finger on the 5th/A string. Play it as you normally would,
but when you get to the 3rd/G string right before the root note, you will want to play the root note with your
pointer finger and that is the transition. So to reiterate, rather than playing the second root note with your pinky
finger, you will want to move your hand position to allow you to play that root note with your pointer finger, then
follow the rest of the pattern.

When descending on the pattern the shift will be similar, when you get to the root note on the 3rd string,
play the root note with your pointer finger then shift your hand so that you play the next note with you ring
finger and continue descending.

IMPORTANT NOTE: The idea with these 5th root patterns with hand position shifts is to allow the player to
reach higher notes but it should sound like there is no shift in hand position. My advice to you is to nail
that shift in hand position. The listener should not be able to tell that you shifted positions. In fact
once you feel comfortable with the shift, do it without looking at the guitar, perfect this so that you can
do it without any problems
