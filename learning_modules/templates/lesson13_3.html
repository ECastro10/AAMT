Arpeggios!

Time for one of my favorite lessons, arpeggios.
An arpeggio is the spelling out of a chord. What do I mean by that? Well let's talk about that.

Concept of an Arpeggio

Suppose you take the word "dollar", to spell the word "dollar", you would spell it like so
"d - o - l - l - a - r". Easy right?
Well that is all an arpeggio is. You take a chord and spell it out. A chord is when you play
a group of notes together at the same time, an arpeggio is basically spelling out that chord by playing
each note in that chord sequentially rather than at the same time.

We will discuss 7ths arpeggios because they go over our basic triads (excluding suspended chords).
We will go over arpeggios with the root on the 6th and 5th string.

Major 7th Arpeggio

Remember the formula for a Major 7th? Well, if you don't, make it a point to memorize them.
FORMULA: 1 - 3 - 5 - 7

As usual all our patterns will span two octaves.
This pattern requires no pattern because it stays confined to four frets. Play this pattern 6th - 1st string (low E -- > high E)
then back down. The idea is to build muscle memory and speed. These arpeggios might be your bread and butter.
One last note before we continue on, once you get the hang of playing these at moderate speeds, try to visualize
scales underneath these arpeggios to break out of the confines of arpeggio patterns.
These exercises should keep you occupied for a while.

Now, the 5th string root pattern.



Alright, the shift here occurs on the 3rd string == g string.
You will play the note before the root with your ring finger, then the root with your pointer.
This breaks the convention a bit because you have to play the root and the following 3rd with the same finger
but this is efficient because your finger does not have to shift position.

When playing back down (high E to A), the shift is identical except in reverse.

Dominant 7th Arpeggio

FORMULA: 1 - 3 - 5 - b7

As you have noticed, this pattern spans 5 frets. That means a possible shift.
You can stretch rather than shift, but shifting makes life easier.
The 6th and 5th strings are played normally, but you should shift on the 4th string == D string.
Shift up the fretboard one fret to be able to play that note before the root with your pointer finger.
The shift should be the same but in reverse playing back down to the 6th string.



The shift on the dom7 arpeggio is identical to the one on major7 arpeggio. The root note in between
both octaves should be played with the pointer finger.

Minor 7th Arpeggio

FORMULA: 1 - b3 - 5 - b7

This pattern requires no shift but it does require the barring of two strings or shifting of pressure,
whatever you choose to call it.



The shift on this pattern occurs on the 2nd string == B string. Play from the root up to the 2nd string.
On the 2nd string, play that first note with your pointer finger. After that, stretch that pinky out to
get that next note. The last two notes on the 1st string might be a challenge to maneuver after the stretch.
I recommend practicing the 2nd and 1st string by themselves, going up and down until your fingers get
comfortable maneuvering the stretch and the notes nearby. Good luck.

Minor 7 Flat 5 Arpeggio

FORMULA: 1 - b3 - b5 - b7

Similar to previous patterns, you will need to barre two strings to get fluid and even sound.



This shift is similar to the minor 7th, 5th string root pattern.
However, I noticed that if I played that note on the 2nd string that is in the fret between the two notes
on the first string, that my pinky had some issues. If you have this issue I recommend playing that note with
your ring finger so that your middle and pinky fingers can move independently around the ring finger.

NOTES: Practice these slowly until you can play them without looking at your guitar or even without thinking.
Once you get that down, increase the speed. Besides sounding fun, they give you some ground
on what to play over chords, because if someone is playing an Amin7, you can just play around the arpeggio
and it will not sound bad.
Try playing these with the same root to hear and feel the difference.
