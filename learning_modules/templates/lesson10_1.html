Natural Minor Scale

Minor scales are to be practiced just like major scales. There will be some shifting on some of the minor scales
so look out for that, but otherwise minor scales are straightforward.

Natural Minor Scale

Formula: Whole - Half - Whole - Whole - Half - Whole - Whole == W, h, W, W, h, W, W
example: A natural minor == A, B, C, D, E, F, G, A

Use this formula to create any natural minor scale.
Now let's see how a A natural minor looks like on a staff.

Now let's take a look at the two octave pattern on our fretboard.

Playing the pattern

Play the 6th and 5th strings with your pointer, ring, and pinky finger. When you get to the 4th string, play
the 7th note with your pointer finger and then the root note with your pinky finger. This constitutes the shift>
Play the 3rd string with your pointer, middle and pinky finger. Shift your hand again by playing the next note
with your pointer finger and play normally for the remainder of the notes.
So the shift is the same playing back to the 6th string. The idea is that the shift only occurs for the 3rd string.

Relative Minors

Notice how the A minor scale is comprised of only whole notes and no accidentals?
If a minor scale has the same notes as a major scale it is most likely the relative minor of that key.
A rule of thumb for finding the relative minor to any key is by going to the 6th scale degree.
A scale degree simply means where on that scale.

example: Key of C: C == 1st scale degree, D == 2nd scale degree, E == 3rd scale degree, ..., A == 6th scale degree, etc..

If you have already looked up circle of fifths/fourths, then chances are that you have already seen a circle
with relative minors on there. Every key has a relative minor because every key has a major scale with a 6th scale degree.


Notes: When you first start playing the minor scale pattern, you might be tempted to end on what would normally
be the root note in a major scale, but don't be fooled.
Our ears have a strong tendency to resolve, the minor scale can be used as an extension for a major scale but
if you want it to truly sound like a minor scale, treat the red dots as the roots for that sound.

Unlocking More of the Fretboard

As mentioned earlier under the notes, the minor scale can be used as an extension of the major scale if you focus
on the appropriate root note.
