Clefs, Staves, and Ledger Lines

Alright, time to learn about some standard notation so that you can read and write down sheet music.
What's the purpose for standard notation? It shows rhythm better than tab notation can and it
it illustrates the relation between the notes.

Clefs

First and foremost, we will be studying primarily the treble class because the guitar range falls easily in this clef.
Here are some but not all clefs.

So the bottom most clef is known as the f clef or bass clef.
You can remember that it is an f-clef because the swirly bit begins on the line that is an f3.
The next two clefs focus on the same note which is the c4 note. The one above the f clef is the tenor
clef and the one above is the alto clef. The only difference between the two clefs is that the tenor
clef is shifted down hence why the center line is an A4. And last but not least we have the clef that most
guitar players call home, the g clef/ treble clef (notice how treble clef's skinny tail begins at g4).

Now that we understand what clefs are, let's focus on the lines and spacings

Staff/Stave

So the clef cannot exist without the staff/stave and vise versa. The clef gives the reader context
and the staff/stave is the map on which to plot the appropriate notes.
Staves are made of two things, lines and spaces. It is vital that you understand that the lines
and spaces both represent notes.

So you can use these shortcuts that the image shows to remember then but remember, this is just like learning
to read, so give yourself time.
Remember how the clefs above this image had notes that were above the normal staff, these little lines specifically
used for these notes are known as ledger lines

You will find these guys very useful as you write music.
