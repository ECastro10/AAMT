Key Signatures

Remember how the clefs gave the reader context of where in the range lies?
Let's take this a step further. Let's determine which notes are not wrong to play, meaning, that they will always
fit, they may not sound that great, but they fit.
NOTES: There are only as many keys as there are notes.
Outside of the key of C, keys have accidentals that are part of that key rather than the natural note, or both.
We will be going through the keys in the circle of fifths fashion by first ascending
up the sharps and then descending down the flats back to C.

A key is basically the root, this means that everything will be related to the root in some way shape or form.
That being said, the notes that are in a key are dictated by major scales. Take any key and find the major scale
with the key as its root note and there are your notes which should line up with the key signature. Let's begin.

Key of C

Key of C == 0 #'s, 0 b's
C - D - E - F - G - A - B


Key of G

Key of G == 1 #
G - A - B - C - D - E - F#


Key of D

Key of D == 2 #'s
D - E - F# - G - A - B - C#


Key of A

Key of A == 3 #'s
A - B - C# - D - E - F# - G#


Key of E

Key of E == 4 #'s
E - F# - G# - A - B - C# - D#


NOTE:There are three sets of keys that are identical except for their context. That is to say
if we continued this pattern it would look like this

Like colors are the keys that have identical notes, just in different context. F# and Gb line up nicely
but C# and Db, and B and Cb do not.

Key of B == 7 b's
Cb - Db - Eb - Fb - Gb - Ab - Bb

F - G - A - Bb - C - D - E


If you want to go the circle of fourths route, just start from 0 b's and go up b's and down #'s.
It is important to understand that the reason it is either fourths or fifths is because of the relation
between the current key and the next key.

example: C - D - E - F - G - A - B
Notice how if C == 1, G == 5 by simply counting up. Now look at the key of G.

example: G - A - B - C - D - E - F#
Notice how you only count up to four? This is the relation between the keys. Adjacent key's will be the fifth or fourth
depending on the direction that you are going.

Practice writing these down over and over. You will start to notice a pattern that dictates which note is the new flat
or sharp. Here is a little shortcut.

Order of Sharps: F, C, G, D, A, E, B
Order of Flats: B, E, A, D, G, C, F

Think of the order as counters. In the key of G there is only 1 #, that is F whereas In the key of A
there are 3 #'s which happen to be F - C - G. Catching on?
