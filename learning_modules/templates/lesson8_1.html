Cadences

Before we dive into the first cadence, let's discuss what a cadence is.

What is a Cadence?

A cadence is simply a group of chords the resolve a musical piece.
In other terms, a cadence gives you that feeling that the song has reached its end or at least a pause.
Chances are that you have heard at least one of the cadences. Practice them to get their respective feels. This chapter will explore a few cadences in the key of C, but remember to practice them in all keys.

Authentic Cadence

SEQUENCE: V - I
SOUND: Strong Resolution, sense of finality.
The first cadence requires the V chord and the I chord.
The idea is to play the V chord and then the I chord. In the key of C it would be G Major followed by C Major.

Plagal Cadence

SEQUENCE: IV - I
SOUND: Strong resolution but weaker than an authentic cadence
In the Key of C you would play F Major followed by a C Major.

Half Cadence

SEQUENCE: ? - V
SOUND: incomplete or suspended, it is considered a weak cadence that calls for continuation.
The ? means that you can use any chord of any type. Explore any and all possibilities but remember
that you cannot go wrong with any chords in the chord family.
example: E minor to a G Major.

Deceptive Cadence

SEQUENCE: V - VI
SOUND: Deceptive as the name implies. Explained below
The reason that this cadence is effective in being deceptive is because the V chord will always want to resolve
to the I chord. So when you play the V, our ears expect the I. You can substitute the VI chord with others, except the I.
You will notice that some chords just don't fit, when in doubt just fallback onto the VI.
As is, this would be a G Major followed by an A minor.

Interrupted Cadence

SEQUENCE: V - ?m - I
SOUND: Misdirection but ultimately resolves, not a strong resolve as an authentic cadence
The ?m means any minor chord, preferably one in the chord family. The basic idea with this cadence is to
setup an authentic cadence with the V chord, redirect to a minor chord, and return back to the I chord.
example: G Major, to a D minor, to a C Major.

These are just a few cadences that you can utilize when creating your own music. Practice all of them and try
substituting chords to hear how they sound but still adhering to that relative cadence.
