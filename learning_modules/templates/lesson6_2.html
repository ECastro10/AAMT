Triad Chords

Remember the Major 2nd, Major 3rd and Perfect 5th?
Let's put those into play, from here on out we will refer to them in a short hand manner simply as 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th, and 7th.

Triad Chords

The most basic chords besides interval type chords are triads. Interval chords consist of 2 notes, triad chords consist of 3 notes.
So all of the formulas in this chapter will only consist of three notes.

NOTE: Make it a point to memorize all of you triad formulas and patterns because these will be the building
blocks for building on top of these chords.
Every chord will be built from a major scale, so if you haven't already, memorize that major scale formula and those scales.
For the sake of brevity, all chords will be built in the key of C but remember that the other key's chords won't be too far
from the key of C chords in terms of spelling.


Major Chords

First of all, the key of C == C - D - E - F - G - A - B
All major chords are stacked in thirds, like so.

FORMULA: 1 - 3 - 5
Space between 1 and 3 == 4 semitones
Space between 3 and 5 == 3 semitones
SOUND: happy and simple

C Major == C - E - G
F Major == F - A - C
G Major == G - B - D

The idea is to choose a note in the scale and that becomes your 1, that is the note name of the chord.
The reason that they are thirds is because C --> D == 2 semitones == Major 2nd, C --> E == 4 semitones == Major 3rd.
Remember that this is not the case with all notes because the amount of semitones between all the notes are not the same.
In any key, there will be only 3 major chords due to the restrictions of semitones spaces.

 NOTE: You will find out later on why a major chord is a major, it has to do mostly with the sound created by the intervals in the chord.


Minor Chords

FORMULA: 1 - b3 - 5
Spaces between 1 and b3 == 3 semitones
Spaces between b3 and 5 == 4 semitones
SOUND: sad or serious

D minor == D - F - A
E minor == E - G - B
A minor == A - C - E

So the only difference here is that this formula requires that you flatten(b) the third, anytime you see a (b) it means
flatten == down one semitone, and (#) means to sharpen == up one semitone.
There will only be 3 minor chords in a key due to the formula.


Diminished Chords

FORMULA: 1 - b3 - b5
Spaces between 1 and b3 == 3 semitones
Spaces between b3 and b5 == 3 semitones
SOUND: dark and edgy

B diminished == B - D - F

This is basically a minor chord with a flattened 5th. You will only find 1 diminished chord in a key.


Augmented Chords

FORMULA: 1 - 3 - #5
Spaces between 1 and 3 == 4 semitones
Spaces between 3 and #5 == 4 semitones
SOUND: suspenseful

This is basically a major chord with a sharpened 5th. There are no augmented chords that occur naturally in any key.
Augmented chords are unstable and they have a tendency to want to move to another more stable chord.


These formulas form the basis for all triad chords that occur naturally in major scales in any key.
Take the sound description with a grain of salt. The sound of each chord can be interpreted in many ways.
Lastly, write these basic triads out over and over until you know your triads by heart. This will make your life
easier later on. NOTE: The 3rd dictates whether the chord is major or minor, from there you then modify the 5th to find out whether
it is an augmented major or a diminished minor (although saying these things is redundant).
